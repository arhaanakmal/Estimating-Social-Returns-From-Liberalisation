# Estimating Social Returns From Liberalisation
A repository cum replication package for paper titled 'Estimating Social Returns from Liberalisation'

## Abstract

This paper asks whether India's 1991 economic liberalisation accelerated social development beyond what would have occurred in its absence, bringing causal evidence to the Bhagwati–Sen debate. Using the synthetic control method, I construct a weighted combination of 26 non-liberalising developing countries that closely reproduces India's pre-1991 trajectory in infant mortality and adult literacy, then compare India's post-reform outcomes to this counterfactual. I ﬁnd no structural break attributable to liberalisation: the treatment-eﬀect gaps are small and statistically indistinguishable from placebo gaps generated by reassigning the 1991 treatment date to donor countries, with permutation p-values of 0.33 and 0.60 for infant mortality and literacy respectively, and leave-one-out checks corroborate the null. The results are more consistent with the capability approach of Drèze and Sen than with the growth-dividend hypothesis of Bhagwati and Panagariya, suggesting that post-1991 social gains reﬂect broad global trends in health and education rather than liberalisation itself.
